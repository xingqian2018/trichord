# Ways to Put Money in Roth IRA

This document outlines all the methods to contribute or transfer money into a Roth IRA account.

---

## 1. Direct Roth IRA Contribution

```
Income (after-tax)
  → Roth IRA
  → $7,500 limit (2026)
```

**Who can use:** Anyone with earned income below the income limits

**Income limits (2026):**
- Married filing jointly: MAGI < $242,000 (full contribution)
- Single: MAGI < $153,000 (full contribution)

**Contribution limit:**
- $7,500 (standard)
- $8,600 (age 50+)

**Tax treatment:** After-tax contribution, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals

---

## 2. Backdoor Roth IRA

```
Income (after-tax)
  → Traditional IRA ($7,500 non-deductible)
  → Immediately convert to Roth IRA
  → $7,500 limit
```

**Who can use:** High earners who exceed Roth IRA income limits
- Married filing jointly: MAGI > $252,000
- Single: MAGI > $168,000

**Contribution limit:**
- $7,500 (standard)
- $8,600 (age 50+)

**Process:**
1. Contribute to Traditional IRA (non-deductible)
2. Immediately convert to Roth IRA
3. File Form 8606 with taxes

**Important warning:** Pro-rata rule applies if you have existing pre-tax IRA balances

**Tax treatment:** No additional tax if done immediately (minimal tax on earnings)

---

## 3. Roth 401(k) Rollover to Roth IRA

```
Roth 401(k) balance
  → Roll over to Roth IRA (when leaving job or in-service)
  → NO LIMIT on rollover amount
```

**Who can use:** Anyone with a Roth 401(k) who:
- Leaves their job, OR
- Has a plan that allows in-service distributions

**Amount limit:** NO LIMIT - can roll over entire balance

**Tax treatment:** Tax-free transfer (both are Roth accounts)

**Important notes:**
- Five-year rule for earnings withdrawals restarts with new Roth IRA
- Direct rollover recommended to avoid withholding

---

## 4. Mega Backdoor Roth (After-tax 401(k) → Roth IRA)

```
After-tax 401(k) contributions (~$45,500)
  → In-service distribution to Roth IRA
  → ~$45,500 additional in Roth IRA per year
```

**Who can use:** Only if your 401(k) plan allows:
- After-tax (non-Roth) contributions
- In-service distributions to Roth IRA OR in-plan Roth conversions

**Contribution limits (2026):**
- Total 401(k) limit: $70,000 (including all contributions and employer match)
- After maxing Roth 401(k) ($24,500), you can contribute ~$45,500 after-tax
- If age 50+: Total limit is $77,500

**Process:**
1. Max out Roth 401(k): $24,500
2. Contribute after-tax (non-Roth) to 401(k): ~$45,500
3. Immediately convert/distribute to Roth IRA or in-plan Roth 401(k)

**Tax treatment:** Tax-free (or minimal tax on earnings if not done immediately)

**Important notes:**
- Not all 401(k) plans offer this option
- Check with your plan administrator

---

## 5. Traditional 401(k) → Roth IRA Conversion

```
Traditional 401(k) balance (pre-tax)
  → Convert/rollover to Roth IRA
  → NO LIMIT on conversion amount
```

**Who can use:** Anyone with a Traditional 401(k)

**Best timing:**
- Low-income years
- Early retirement (after leaving job, before Social Security)
- Years with large deductions/losses

**Amount limit:** NO LIMIT on conversion amount

**Tax treatment:**
- Pay ordinary income tax on the entire converted amount
- The converted amount is added to your taxable income for the year
- Can push you into higher tax brackets

**Strategic considerations:**
- Consider spreading conversions over multiple years
- Avoid conversions that trigger higher Medicare premiums
- Pay taxes from non-retirement funds if possible

---

## 6. Traditional IRA → Roth IRA Conversion

```
Traditional IRA balance (pre-tax)
  → Convert to Roth IRA
  → NO LIMIT on conversion amount
```

**Who can use:** Anyone with a Traditional IRA

**Best timing:**
- Low-income years
- Before RMDs begin (age 73)
- When tax rates are expected to increase

**Amount limit:** NO LIMIT on conversion amount

**Tax treatment:**
- Pay ordinary income tax on the entire converted amount
- Converted amount is added to your taxable income
- Can do partial conversions to manage tax brackets

**Strategic considerations:**
- Unlike Backdoor Roth IRA (#2), this is for existing pre-tax balances
- Can be done incrementally over multiple years
- Consider tax bracket impact

## Maximum Annual Roth IRA Contributions Summary

### If your 401(k) plan allows Mega Backdoor + in-service distributions:

```
$7,500   - Direct/Backdoor Roth IRA contribution
$45,500  - Mega Backdoor (after-tax 401k → Roth IRA)
─────────
$53,000  - TOTAL into Roth IRA per year
```

### If your 401(k) plan does NOT allow Mega Backdoor:

```
$7,500   - Direct/Backdoor Roth IRA contribution only
```

### Additionally (stays in 401(k) until rollover):

```
$24,500  - Roth 401(k) contributions (can roll to Roth IRA when leaving job)
```

## Key Distinctions

### Backdoor Roth IRA vs Mega Backdoor Roth

| Feature | Backdoor Roth IRA | Mega Backdoor Roth |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Limit** | $7,500 | ~$70,000 total (401k) |
| **Uses** | Traditional IRA | 401(k) after-tax contributions |
| **Availability** | Anyone | Only if 401(k) plan allows |
| **Destination** | Roth IRA | Roth IRA or Roth 401(k) |
| **Tax on conversion** | Minimal (if done quickly) | Minimal (if done quickly) |
| **Main purpose** | Bypass income limits | Maximize Roth contributions |

### Contribution vs Conversion/Rollover

**Contributions** (subject to annual limits):
- Direct Roth IRA: $7,500
- Backdoor Roth IRA: $7,500
- Mega Backdoor: ~$45,500 additional

**Conversions/Rollovers** (NO annual limits):
- Roth 401(k) → Roth IRA: Unlimited
- Traditional 401(k) → Roth IRA: Unlimited (but taxable)
- Traditional IRA → Roth IRA: Unlimited (but taxable)

---

## Important Tax Considerations

### Tax-Free Transfers:
- Roth 401(k) → Roth IRA
- After-tax 401(k) → Roth IRA (if done immediately)
- Direct Roth IRA contribution (already taxed)
- Backdoor Roth IRA (if done immediately)

### Taxable Conversions:
- Traditional 401(k) → Roth IRA (pay income tax)
- Traditional IRA → Roth IRA (pay income tax)
- Pre-tax 401(k) → Roth 401(k) (pay income tax)
